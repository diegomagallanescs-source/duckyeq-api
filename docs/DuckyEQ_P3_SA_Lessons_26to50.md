**Empathic Responsiveness --- Lessons 26--30**

Lessons 26--30 form a dedicated cluster on empathic responsiveness:
staying in the conversation (don\'t change the subject), responding
specifically to what was actually said, going the extra mile, asking
what people need rather than assuming, and following up after the moment
ends. These five lessons directly address the most common empathy
failures --- good intentions without real presence.

Key drill for L26-27: when someone says they are sad about xyz, respond
to xyz. Not to a general version of it. Not with a topic change. To the
specific thing they shared. Stay there. That is the skill.

**Tier 3 (cont.) · Empathic Responsiveness (Levels 26--30)**

Five lessons on practical empathy: staying in the conversation,
responding specifically to what was said, going the extra mile, asking
what someone actually needs, and following up after the conversation
ends.

**Level 26 · Stay In It --- Don\'t Change the Subject**

*Tier 3 · Deep Empathy*

+:---------------------------------------------------------------------:+
| **Core Message --- duck highlights on lesson open**                   |
|                                                                       |
| **When someone shares something real, the most caring thing you can   |
| do is respond to THAT. Stay right there with them. Don\'t move on.**  |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

**Objective:** Build the habit of staying inside what someone shared ---
not pivoting, not redirecting, not jumping to your own story.

**Quack of the Day**

*\[curious\] Why did the duck stop switching topics every time his

*\[playful\] **Because he finally realized: you cannot be WITH someone

  one would it be?

  it or quietly look for a way out?

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **DEFINE** concept + tappable flashcards · duck is SMALL in corner

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Concept:** When someone shares something important --- a fear, a loss,
a struggle --- they are inviting you into it. If your response changes
the subject, pivots to yourself, or skips past it to something lighter,
they feel the invitation get rejected. Staying in it means your response
is ABOUT what they just shared. In it. Not next to it.

*Flashcards (tap-to-advance, duck expression per card):*

**1.** *\[curious\] \"Topic switching sounds like: \'Oh, that\'s rough
--- anyway, did you see what happened yesterday?\' The \'anyway\' is the
exact moment you leave them.\"*

**2.** *\[thoughtful\] \"Staying in it sounds like: \'That sounds really
hard. How long has that been going on?\' Your entire response is about
what they said --- not near it, IN it.\"*

**3.** *\[playful\] \"Someone says \'I\'ve been really stressed about my
mom lately.\' Staying in it: \'What\'s going on with her?\' Leaving it:
\'Ugh, parents are the worst. Mine did this thing last week\...\'\"*

**4.** *\[calm\] \"Why we pivot: it makes us uncomfortable to sit with
someone\'s pain. Pivoting is often self-protection wearing the costume
of a response. The other person always feels it.\"*

**5.** *\[surprised\] \"Research shows that when people feel their topic
was changed mid-vulnerability, they are 40% less likely to share again
with that same person. One pivot costs a lot of trust.\"*

**6.** *\[happy\] \"Staying in it just requires asking one more question
about what they said --- before you say anything about yourself.\"*

**7.** *\[encouraging\] \"The rule: your response should include
something specific from what they just shared. If it could have been
said about anything, it was not really a response.\"*

**6.** *\[happy\] \"You do not have to get it perfectly. Even a question

**7.** *\[encouraging\] \"Specificity is how you prove you were there.

**Level 29 · Ask What They Need --- Not What You Think They Need**

*Tier 3 · Deep Empathy*

+:---------------------------------------------------------------------:+
| **Core Message --- duck highlights on lesson open**                   |
|                                                                       |
| **The most empathetic thing you can ask someone is: what do you       |
| actually need right now? Not what YOU think they need.**              |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

**Objective:** Learn to ask before you help --- and understand why the
right kind of support matters as much as the amount of it.

**Quack of the Day**

*\[curious\] Why did the duck stop deciding what was best for his friend
without asking?*

*\[playful\] **Because his friend needed presence and the duck kept
showing up with solutions --- and after a while his friend stopped
calling!***

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **CONNECT** 5 questions · duck is LARGE on screen · answers never
  stored

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

- Have you ever tried to help someone in the way YOU thought was best
  --- only to find out they needed something completely different?

- Has anyone ever tried to help you in a way that completely missed what
  you actually needed?

- When you are struggling, what do you most often need that people tend
  not to give you?

- Random: if you could tell the people who love you one thing you
  actually need that they don\'t know, what would it be?

- Do you find it easy or hard to ask for what you need when you are the
  one who is struggling?

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **DEFINE** concept + tappable flashcards · duck is SMALL in corner

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Concept:** People in pain know what they need better than anyone
outside them does. The problem is they are often not asked. Instead they
receive advice when they needed silence, practical help when they needed
company, or space when they were hoping someone would stay. Four words
change all of this: \'What do you need?\'

*Flashcards (tap-to-advance, duck expression per card):*

**1.** *\[curious\] \"Two main types of support: emotional (someone to
hear you and be with you) and practical (someone to help fix the actual
problem). They are different needs. Ask which one is needed first.\"*

**2.** *\[thoughtful\] \"A useful question: \'Do you want me to just
listen, or would it help to think through some options?\' This one
question prevents giving the wrong kind of help entirely.\"*

**3.** *\[playful\] \"I once made a whole plan to fix a duck\'s problem.
She just wanted someone to swim beside her quietly. My entire plan was
useless. The swimming was what she needed.\"*

**4.** *\[calm\] \"When someone does not know what they need --- common
when overwhelmed --- offer two options: \'Would it help to talk it
through, or would it help to just not be alone right now?\'\"*

**5.** *\[surprised\] \"Research: people who receive the TYPE of support
they actually wanted recover faster than people who receive more support
of the wrong type.\"*

**6.** *\[happy\] \"Asking what someone needs also respects their
agency. It treats them as someone who knows their own experience --- not
someone you need to rescue from the outside.\"*

**7.** *\[encouraging\] \"Four words that change everything: \'What do
you need?\' Simple. Direct. Completely respectful. Try them this
week.\"*

**Level 30 · The Follow-Up --- Empathy That Comes Back**

*Tier 3 · Deep Empathy*

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

**Objective:** Build the habit of following up after hard conversations
--- and understand why it is one of the highest-impact, lowest-cost
things you can do.

**Quack of the Day**

*\[curious\] Why did the duck send a message three days after a hard

*\[playful\] **Because empathy that only shows up for the event and
disappears right after is just great acting --- not actual caring!***

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **CONNECT** 5 questions · duck is LARGE on screen · answers never

- Do you tend to follow up after someone shares something hard --- or

- Is there someone you talked to recently who deserves a follow-up you
  have not sent?

- Random: what is the most meaningful follow-up anyone has ever given

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **DEFINE** concept + tappable flashcards · duck is SMALL in corner

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Concept:** Most empathy is focused on the moment. Someone shares
something painful. You respond well. The conversation ends. And then
life moves on --- for you. But for them, the thing they shared often
does not end. Following up --- even once, even briefly --- closes that
gap in a way that nothing else can.

*Flashcards (tap-to-advance, duck expression per card):*

**1.** *\[curious\] \"A follow-up message says three things at once: I

not have to wait for the \'right moment.\' Just send it.\"*

**3.** *\[playful\] \"The easiest follow-up in the world: \'Hey, I\'ve
been thinking about what you shared the other day. How are you doing
with it?\' Thirty words. Enormous weight.\"*

**4.** *\[calm\] \"Following up does not require having anything new to
say. \'Just checking in on you\' with no advice attached is one of the
most caring messages you can send.\"*

**5.** *\[surprised\] \"Research shows follow-up contact after a
difficult disclosure significantly reduces feelings of isolation --- and
whether it happens matters more than exactly when.\"*

**6.** *\[happy\] \"If you are worried you will forget: set a reminder
the moment the conversation ends. \'Check in with \[name\] Friday.\' Put
it in your phone immediately.\"*

**7.** *\[encouraging\] \"The people in your life who follow up are the
ones you trust most. You know they actually mean it. Be one of those
people.\"*
