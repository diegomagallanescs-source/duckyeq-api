**Objective:** Learn how to respond responsibly when rules feel unfair

**Quack of the Day**

*\[curious\] Why did the duck say that \'the rule seems unfair\' was the
beginning of the thinking --- not the end of it?*

*\[playful\] **Because deciding to break every rule that felt
uncomfortable turned out to look a lot less like justice and a lot more

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **CONNECT** 5 questions · duck is LARGE on screen · answers never

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

- Have you ever felt a rule was unfair? What did you do?

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **DEFINE** concept + tappable flashcards · duck is SMALL in corner

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Concept:** Some rules are unfair. Some are just inconvenient. Telling
the difference --- and responding thoughtfully --- is the skill. Before
deciding a rule is wrong, run the full Stoplight. Ask: is this rule
actually unjust, or am I just frustrated by it? Then decide what to do
next --- and do it responsibly.

*Flashcards (tap-to-advance, duck expression per card):*

**1.** *\[curious\] \"Questions to ask about an unfair rule: Who does
this rule protect? Who does it harm? Is there a reason behind it I\'m
not seeing? And importantly: is it inconvenient to ME or actually unjust
to SOMEONE?\"*

**2.** *\[thoughtful\] \"Responsible ways to address unfair rules: name
your concern to someone who can change it, ask questions through the
right channels, follow it while advocating to change it, or in serious
cases of genuine injustice --- civil disobedience (accepting the
consequences of breaking it deliberately).\"*

**3.** *\[playful\] \"Breaking a rule is a decision. It has
consequences. Responsible rule-breaking means: you have thought it
through, you know why, you stand behind it, and you accept what
follows.\"*

**4.** *\[calm\] \"Not every uncomfortable rule deserves to be broken.
Some rules exist for reasons we don\'t fully see yet. Applying the
Stoplight before deciding --- especially on this one --- matters a
lot.\"*

**5.** *\[happy\] \"The history of change is full of people who said
\'this rule is wrong\' and worked to change it --- responsibly,
deliberately, and with clear values behind them. That is responsible
decision-making at its bravest.\"*

**6.** *\[surprised\] \"Research on moral development (Kohlberg) shows
that the highest level of ethical thinking involves reasoning from
principles --- not just following rules OR ignoring them. It is the
hardest level. It is also the most important.\"*

**7.** *\[encouraging\] \"Not every rule is right. Not every rule should
be broken. The skill is knowing which is which --- and acting
responsibly either way.\"*

  **ENGAGE** the game (scored 0--300) · duck is TINY / HIDDEN

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Game:** Emotion Mixer Lab

**Scenario:** Mix \'rule situation\' + \'motivation\' beakers to
